Immediate implant placement in the aesthetic zone is one of those procedures where the one-year results look brilliant in the literature, but we've always been a bit short on long-term soft tissue data. This five-year prospective study from Chen and Buser's group starts to fill that gap — and the findings are worth paying attention to.

## The study

Forty-two patients received immediate implants in the upper anterior region, all with simultaneous contour augmentation using a xenograft and either a connective tissue graft or collagen matrix. The follow-up protocol was rigorous: standardised photography, PES scores, and mid-facial mucosal level measurements at 1, 3, and 5 years.

## What happened over time

At one year, the results were broadly excellent — consistent with what we've come to expect from well-executed immediate placement protocols. PES scores were high, patients were happy, and mid-facial mucosal levels were stable or even slightly improved from baseline.

By year five, though, a clear pattern emerged. Around 60% of sites showed some degree of mid-facial recession compared to the one-year measurement. The mean was modest — roughly 0.5mm — but some sites lost close to 1.5mm. The connective tissue graft group held up slightly better than the collagen matrix group, but neither was immune.

## Why this matters

We've been telling patients (and ourselves) that immediate placement with grafting gives predictable aesthetic results. And it does — for the first couple of years. This paper suggests that the soft tissue continues to remodel well beyond our usual follow-up window.

For high-stakes aesthetic cases — thin biotype, high smile line, a patient who notices every detail — this five-year data argues for more conservative expectations at the consent stage. It doesn't mean we shouldn't place immediate implants in the aesthetic zone. It means we should stop presenting the one-year photo as the final result.
