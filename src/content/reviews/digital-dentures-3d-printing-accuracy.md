The promise of 3D-printed dentures has been floating around for years now, but most of us have had the same nagging question: sure, they're faster — but are they actually accurate enough to rely on?

This systematic review pulls together 23 studies examining the trueness (how close to the intended shape) and precision (how repeatable across prints) of 3D-printed complete denture bases. It's the kind of paper that saves you reading two dozen individual studies, which is exactly why it caught my eye.

## What they found

The pooled trueness values came in well within what most of us would consider clinically acceptable — generally under 100μm of deviation from the digital design. That's comparable to milled denture bases, which has been the gold standard for CAD/CAM dentures up to this point.

Precision, though, was more variable. Some printer-resin combinations produced highly repeatable results, while others showed concerning spread. The type of 3D printing technology mattered: DLP printers tended to outperform FDM, and the specific resin used had as much impact on accuracy as the hardware itself.

## The catch

What struck me was how much the post-processing protocol influenced outcomes. Studies that carefully controlled UV curing times and support removal showed tighter numbers. The ones that didn't — or didn't report their post-processing steps in detail — had wider variance.

This is the gap between the technology working in a controlled study and working reliably in your lab on a Tuesday afternoon. The machine is only as good as the workflow around it.

## What it means in practice

If you're already using or considering a digital denture workflow, this is reassuring data. The accuracy is there — in principle. But the meta-analysis makes clear that you can't treat all 3D printers and resins as interchangeable. Your specific combination needs validation, ideally against a milled or conventionally processed reference.

For those of us who've been gradually shifting toward digital prosthodontics, this paper doesn't change the direction — it just sharpens the implementation details.
