The occlusal splint literature has spent two decades asking which splint material wears best, as though the splint exists in a vacuum rather than in a mouth full of materials it will spend every night grinding against. The antagonist (enamel, lithium disilicate, 4Y-TZP zirconia) has largely been treated as a background condition, something the splint simply encounters. Arreaza and colleagues at Ohio State University, in "Comparison of wear behavior of occlusal device materials manufactured by different processes," have inverted the frame. Their finding, delivered with the quiet authority of a 2-way ANOVA, is that what opposes the splint matters more than what the splint is made from. The clinical implication runs deeper than material science: it has something to say about restoration planning in patients who wear splints.

### The Data Anchor

This was a controlled in-vitro study comparing three splint materials (milled PMMA, ProArt CAD Splint; 3D-printed resin, NextDent Ortho Rigid; heat-polymerised PMMA, Vitacrilic Clear) against three antagonists (human enamel, IPS e.max ZirCAD MT zirconia, IPS e.max CAD lithium disilicate). Fifteen Ø14×2 mm discs were fabricated per splint material, divided into five-specimen subgroups per antagonist. The OHSU oral wear simulator delivered 50,000 mastication cycles at 1.1 Hz and 50 N, simulating roughly six to twelve months of clinical function. Volumetric wear was quantified using a 3Shape E4 scanner and WearCompare software; statistical analysis used a 2-way ANOVA with Tukey-Kramer HSD post hoc testing at α = .025.

### Key Findings

- **The antagonist material was the dominant variable.** Antagonist type significantly influenced splint wear (P < .001), while the splint material itself did not reach significance at the study threshold (P = .029, with α = .025). The interaction between substrate and antagonist was non-significant (P = .42).
- **Printed resin showed the least wear** across antagonist groups; the highest substrate loss occurred when milled PMMA (MMA) was opposed by lithium disilicate (LD-MMA group), which differed significantly from the printed resin groups.
- **Human enamel caused significantly more substrate wear than lithium disilicate** (P < .01 on pairwise testing) — a somewhat counterintuitive result that reflects enamel's capacity for abrasive surface contact.
- **Lithium disilicate suffered the most antagonist wear.** Among the three opposing materials, LD lost significantly more volume than either enamel or zirconia (both P < .01), consistent with its lower fracture toughness and susceptibility to abrasive wear compared with zirconia.
- **Zirconia and enamel performed similarly as antagonists** — no significant difference in their own volume loss (P = .993).
- **Printed resin's wear resistance may reflect crosslinking chemistry.** Despite lower degree of monomer conversion (~58%) compared with heat-polymerised PMMA (~95%), the crosslinked polymer architecture of NextDent Ortho Rigid appears to confer superior wear resistance; degree of conversion is not the only structural determinant.
- **Limitation: in-vitro conditions.** The OHSU machine replicates 6–12 months of wear in a controlled environment; it cannot replicate the variability of parafunctional behaviour, salivary chemistry, or restoration surface finish in clinical use.

> The 3D printing advocates will reasonably claim vindication here, though the result also raises a question: if the choice of splint material is secondary to what it opposes, is the clinical conversation around CAD-CAM and printed splints placing its emphasis in the right place?

### 💡 The Clinical Bottom Line

For patients with bruxism and lithium disilicate restorations, this paper offers a quietly uncomfortable reframe: the splint is protecting those restorations, yes, but at a cost to itself that is meaningfully higher than if the same patient had zirconia or natural enamel opposing. **Printed splint materials (particularly NextDent Ortho Rigid) appear to be the most wear-resistant option across antagonist types** and may be preferable for high-load bruxing patients. The more provocative implication, however, belongs to the restoration side of the ledger. Before placing lithium disilicate in a confirmed bruxer, it is worth knowing that even the splint designed to protect it will wear faster against it. Choosing materials in a bruxing patient is less about optimising any single component and more about understanding the wear ecology of the whole mouth.
